## 1. Judicial Appointments - Supreme Court

### Second Judges Case (1993) - Supreme Court Advocates-on-Record Association vs Union of India

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**Background:**
- First Judges Case (1981) - President has primacy in judicial appointments
- Second Judges Case (1993) - **Collegium System established**
- Overruled First Judges Case

#### **Collegium System Introduced:**

**For Chief Justice of India (CJI):**
- Appointed by President
- Based on principle of **seniority**
- Consultation with collegium required

**For Supreme Court Judges:**
- **Collegium composition:** CJI + 4 senior-most judges of SC
- Recommendation binding on government
- President must appoint as per collegium's recommendation

**For High Court Chief Justice:**
- Collegium: CJI + 2 senior-most SC judges
- Consultation with Governor and outgoing Chief Justice

**For High Court Judges:**
- HC Collegium: Chief Justice of HC + 2 senior-most judges
- Approval from SC Collegium required

#### ðŸ’¡ Brainstorming Points

**Why was Collegium System needed?**
- Ensure **judicial independence**
- Prevent executive interference
- Maintain separation of powers

**Criticisms:**
- Lack of transparency
- No accountability
- Nepotism concerns
- Opaque selection process

**NJAC (National Judicial Appointments Commission):**
- 99th Constitutional Amendment (2014)
- Struck down by SC in 2015 (Fourth Judges Case)
- Violated basic structure - judicial independence

#### âœ… Revision Checklist
- [ ] Second Judges Case = Collegium System
- [ ] SC Collegium = CJI + 4 senior judges
- [ ] HC Chief Justice = CJI + 2 senior SC judges
- [ ] NJAC struck down in 2015
- [ ] Seniority principle for CJI appointment

- **Article 217** - Appointment of HC judges
- **Article 222** - Transfer of HC judges

## 2. Supreme Court Advisory Jurisdiction

### Constitutional Provision: Article 143

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**What is Advisory Jurisdiction?**
- President can seek SC's **opinion** on questions of law or fact
- Of **public importance**
- Arisen or likely to arise

#### **Characteristics:**

1. **Discretionary Power of President:**
   - President "may" refer (not "shall")
   - Not mandatory

2. **Supreme Court's Discretion:**
   - SC may refuse to give opinion
   - Opinion is **advisory, not binding**

3. **Matters that can be referred:**
   - Questions of law
   - Questions of fact
   - Pre-decided questions (disputes)
   - Hypothetical questions

4. **Nature of Opinion:**
   - Not a judgment/decree
   - Not binding on parties
   - Not enforceable like a decree
   - However, has **persuasive value**

#### ðŸ’¡ Brainstorming Points

**Why Advisory Jurisdiction?**
- Seek expert opinion on complex legal matters
- Prevent future constitutional conflicts
- Guide executive in policy matters

**Important Cases:**
- **Berubari Union Case (1960)** - First major use
- **Cauvery Water Dispute (1992)**
- **Ram Janmabhoomi Case (1993)** - SC declined to answer
- **Special Reference No. 1 of 2012** - 2G spectrum allocation

**Difference from Regular Jurisdiction:**
| Aspect | Advisory | Regular |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Nature | Opinion | Judgment |
| Binding | No | Yes |
| Enforcement | Cannot | Can |
| Parties | No specific | Specific parties |

#### âœ… Revision Checklist
- [ ] Article 143 - Advisory jurisdiction
- [ ] President "may" refer
- [ ] Opinion is NOT binding
- [ ] SC can refuse to give opinion
- [ ] First major case - Berubari Union (1960)

#### ðŸ”— Related Articles
- **Article 131** - Original jurisdiction
- **Article 136** - Special Leave Petition

## 3. No Confidence Motion

### Constitutional Provision: Article 75(3) & Rule 198 of Lok Sabha Rules

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**What is No Confidence Motion?**
- Parliamentary device to remove Council of Ministers
- Tests majority support in Lok Sabha
- Based on **collective responsibility** principle

#### **Key Features:**

1. **Only in Lok Sabha:**
   - Cannot be moved in Rajya Sabha
   - Against Council of Ministers as a whole

2. **Support Required:**
   - At least **50 members** must support the motion
   - For admission

3. **Speaker's Role:**
   - Decides admission
   - Fixes date for discussion (within 10 days)

4. **Discussion:**
   - No time limit for discussion
   - All aspects of government can be discussed
   - Ministers can participate

5. **Voting:**
   - Simple majority to pass
   - If passed, entire Council of Ministers must resign

#### **Difference: No Confidence vs Censure Motion**

| Aspect | No Confidence | Censure Motion |
|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Against | Council of Ministers | Specific Minister/Policy |
| Reasons | Not stated | Must state reasons |
| Consequence | Resignation mandatory | Moral pressure only |
| Voting | Required | Required |

#### ðŸ’¡ Brainstorming Points

**Why only in Lok Sabha?**
- Lok Sabha represents people directly
- Council of Ministers responsible to Lok Sabha (Art 75(3))
- Rajya Sabha is permanent body

**Historical Examples:**
- First successful: Morarji Desai govt (1979)
- V.P. Singh govt (1990)
- H.D. Deve Gowda (1997)
- Atal Bihari Vajpayee (1999)

**Anti-Defection Law Impact:**
- Makes no-confidence difficult
- Whip can be issued
- Defecting members disqualified

#### âœ… Revision Checklist
- [ ] Only in Lok Sabha (not Rajya Sabha)
- [ ] 50 members support needed
- [ ] Based on collective responsibility (Art 75(3))
- [ ] Simple majority to pass
- [ ] Discussion within 10 days of admission
- [ ] No reasons need to be stated

#### ðŸ”— Related Articles
- **Article 75(3)** - Collective responsibility
- **Article 118** - Parliamentary procedure
- **10th Schedule** - Anti-defection

## 4. Appointment and Removal of Ministers

## A. CENTRAL MINISTERS

### Constitutional Provisions: Articles 74, 75

#### ðŸŽ¯ Appointment Process

**1. Prime Minister:**
- Appointed by **President** (Art 75(1))
- Leader of majority party/coalition in Lok Sabha
- No formal qualification prescribed

**2. Other Ministers:**
- Appointed by President on **advice of PM** (Art 75(1))
- PM has complete discretion
- Usually from Parliament (Lok Sabha/Rajya Sabha)

**3. Six-Month Rule:**
- If not MP, must become MP within **6 months** (Art 75(5))
- Otherwise, ceases to be minister

**4. Types of Ministers:**
- **Cabinet Ministers** - Senior, policy-making
- **Ministers of State (Independent charge)** - Head departments
- **Ministers of State** - Assist Cabinet Ministers
- **Deputy Ministers** (discontinued since 1967)

#### ðŸŽ¯ Removal Process

**Methods of Removal:**

1. **By Prime Minister:**
   - Can **advise President** to remove any minister
   - President bound to follow advice
   - Most common method

2. **Collective Resignation:**
   - If PM resigns, entire Council of Ministers resigns
   - Death of PM also leads to dissolution of Council

3. **No Confidence Motion:**
   - Passed in Lok Sabha
   - Entire Council must resign

4. **Individual Minister Removal:**
   - President can dismiss on PM's advice
   - No Parliamentary approval needed

#### ðŸ’¡ Key Principle: Ministers hold office during **pleasure of President** (Art 75(2))
- But in reality, during pleasure of PM

## B. STATE MINISTERS

### Constitutional Provisions: Articles 163, 164

#### ðŸŽ¯ Appointment Process

**1. Chief Minister:**
- Appointed by **Governor** (Art 164(1))
- Leader of majority party/coalition in Legislative Assembly
- No formal qualification

**2. Other Ministers:**
- Appointed by Governor on **advice of CM** (Art 164(1))
- CM has complete discretion
- Usually from State Legislature

**3. Six-Month Rule:**
- Must be member of State Legislature within **6 months** (Art 164(4))

**4. Size Restriction:**
- Total ministers (including CM) cannot exceed **15% of total strength** of Legislative Assembly
- Minimum: **12 ministers** (if 15% is less than 12) - 91st Amendment

#### ðŸŽ¯ Removal Process

**Methods:**

1. **By Chief Minister:**
   - Can advise Governor to remove any minister
   - Governor bound to follow

2. **Collective Resignation:**
   - If CM resigns/dies, entire Council resigns
   - After Assembly dissolution

3. **No Confidence in Assembly:**
   - Entire Council must resign

4. **Individual Removal:**
   - Governor can dismiss on CM's advice

#### ðŸ’¡ Key Principle: Ministers hold office during **pleasure of Governor** (Art 164(1))
- But actually, during pleasure of CM

## 5. Contempt of Court

### Constitutional Provision: Article 129 (SC) & Article 215 (HC)

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**What is Contempt of Court?**
- Any act that **obstructs** or **tends to obstruct** administration of justice
- **Scandalizes** or **lowers** the authority of court
- Prejudices judicial proceedings

**Why Contempt Power?**
- Protect **dignity** and **authority** of courts
- Ensure **fearless** and **impartial** justice delivery
- Maintain **public confidence** in judiciary
- Courts are custodians of Rule of Law

#### **Types of Contempt:**

### 1. Civil Contempt
- **Willful disobedience** of court order/judgment
- Breach of undertaking to court
- Example: Not complying with court order

### 2. Criminal Contempt
- Publication/action that:
  - Scandalizes or lowers authority of court
  - Prejudices judicial proceedings
  - Interferes with administration of justice

**Forms:**
- Words (written/spoken)
- Signs/visible representation
- Actions

#### **Contempt of Courts Act, 1971**

**Key Provisions:**

**Section 2(c)** - Definition of criminal contempt

**Section 12** - Defences available:
1. **Truth as defence** - If in public interest and act was bona fide
2. **Fair criticism** of judicial act
3. **Fair comment** on merits of decided case

**Punishment:**
- **Simple imprisonment** - up to 6 months
- **Fine** - up to Rs 2,000
- **Both**

**Limitation:**
- Contempt proceedings must be initiated within **1 year**

#### **Who Can Punish?**

1. **Supreme Court:**
   - Article 129 - Court of Record
   - Suo motu power to punish
   - Can punish contempt of itself

2. **High Courts:**
   - Article 215 - Court of Record
   - Can punish contempt of itself and subordinate courts

3. **Subordinate Courts:**
   - No inherent power
   - Can only report to HC

#### ðŸ’¡ Brainstorming Points

**Important Cases:**

1. **E.M. Sankaran Namboodiripad vs T. Narayanan Nambiar (1970)**
   - Fair criticism vs contempt

2. **Arundhati Roy Case (2002)**
   - Symbolic sentence (1 day + Rs 2,000)

3. **Prashant Bhushan Case (2020)**
   - Tweets about SC
   - Convicted but symbolic punishment

4. **Attorney General of India vs Oswald Joseph (2021)**
   - Contempt for tweet during farmers' protest
   - SC said freedom of speech must be protected

**Balance with Freedom of Speech:**
- Article 19(1)(a) - Freedom of speech
- Article 19(2) - Reasonable restrictions
- Courts must balance both
- Fair criticism allowed
- Malicious/scandalous statements = contempt

**What is NOT Contempt?**
- Fair and reasonable criticism
- Pointing out judicial errors (constructive)
- Academic discussion
- Reporting of proceedings

#### âœ… Revision Checklist
- [ ] Article 129 (SC) & 215 (HC) - Court of Record
- [ ] Two types: Civil & Criminal contempt
- [ ] Punishment: 6 months jail + Rs 2,000 fine
- [ ] Contempt of Courts Act, 1971
- [ ] Truth as defence (in public interest)
- [ ] 1 year limitation period
- [ ] Protects dignity and authority of courts
- [ ] Subordinate courts report to HC

#### ðŸ”— Related Articles
- **Article 129** - SC as Court of Record
- **Article 215** - HC as Court of Record
- **Article 19(1)(a)** - Freedom of Speech
- **Article 19(2)** - Reasonable restrictions

## 7. Removal of High Court Judge

### Constitutional Provision: Article 124(4), 124(5), 217, 218

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**How HC Judge is Removed:**
- **Same procedure** as SC judge removal
- Process called **IMPEACHMENT**
- One of the most difficult removal procedures

#### **Step-by-Step Impeachment Process:**

### Step 1: Motion Introduction

**In Lok Sabha:**
- **100 members** must sign removal motion
- Given to **Speaker**

**In Rajya Sabha:**
- **50 members** must sign removal motion  
- Given to **Chairman**

### Step 2: Speaker/Chairman Decision
- Admits or rejects motion
- If admits, constitutes investigation committee

### Step 3: Investigation Committee
**Composition:**
- **3 members:**
  1. Sitting Supreme Court Judge
  2. Sitting High Court Chief Justice
  3. Distinguished Jurist

**Function:**
- Investigates charges
- Submits report to Speaker/Chairman

### Step 4: Discussion in House
- If committee finds judge guilty
- Motion discussed in originating House

### Step 5: Voting in First House
- **Special Majority required:**
  - Majority of **total membership**
  - **2/3rd majority** of members present and voting
- If passed, sent to other House

### Step 6: Voting in Second House
- Same **special majority** required
- If passed, sent to President

### Step 7: President's Order
- President **must remove** the judge
- No discretion
- Issues removal order

---

### ðŸ“Š Grounds for Removal

**Two Grounds Only:**

1. **Proved Misbehaviour:**
   - Misconduct
   - Financial irregularities
   - Moral turpitude
   - Actions unbecoming of a judge

2. **Incapacity:**
   - Physical/mental incapacity
   - Inability to perform duties

---

### ðŸ’¡ Brainstorming Points

**Why Such Difficult Procedure?**
- Ensure **judicial independence**
- Prevent frivolous allegations
- Protect judges from political pressure
- Only serious misconduct should lead to removal

**Important Facts:**
- **NO HC judge has ever been removed** through impeachment
- Few attempts made, none successful

**Famous Cases:**

1. **Justice V. Ramaswami (1993)**
   - First impeachment motion in independent India
   - SC judge
   - Motion failed in Lok Sabha (Congress abstained)
   - Not passed

2. **Justice Soumitra Sen (2011)**
   - Calcutta High Court judge
   - First judge against whom motion was passed
   - Resigned before removal order
   - Technically not removed

3. **Justice P.D. Dinakaran (2011)**
   - Karnataka HC Chief Justice
   - Impeachment motion initiated
   - Resigned before proceedings

**HC Judge vs SC Judge Removal:**
- **Same procedure** for both
- Article 124 applies to HC judges through Article 218
- Equal protection ensures independence

---

### ðŸ“‹ Comparison: Different Removal Procedures

| Position | Grounds | Procedure | Majority |
|----------|---------|-----------|----------|
| **HC/SC Judge** | Misbehaviour, Incapacity | Impeachment | Special (2/3 + majority of total) |
| **President/VP** | Violation of Constitution | Impeachment | Special (2/3 + majority of total) |
| **CAG** | Misbehaviour, Incapacity | Like SC Judge | Special |
| **CEC** | Misbehaviour, Incapacity | Like SC Judge | Special |
| **Governor** | None needed | President's pleasure | No vote needed |
| **Minister** | None needed | PM/CM's advice | No vote needed |

## 8. Types of Constitutional Amendments

### Constitutional Provision: Article 368

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**Constitutional Amendment Process:**
- India has **flexible-cum-rigid** constitution
- Some provisions easy to amend, others difficult
- **Parliament** has primary power to amend

---

### ðŸ“Š Three Types of Amendments

## TYPE 1: Simple Majority

**Process:**
- Bill passed in each House by **simple majority**
- Majority of members **present and voting**
- President's assent required
- **No special procedure**

**Matters Amended (Examples):**
- Creation/abolition of Legislative Councils (Art 169)
- Citizenship provisions (Art 5-11)
- Elections to Parliament & State Legislatures
- Union Territories (Art 239-242)
- 5th Schedule (Scheduled Areas)
- 6th Schedule (Tribal Areas)
- Administration of UTs
- Use of English language in Parliament/Courts
- Salaries & allowances of President, Governors
- Quorum in Parliament

**Key Characteristics:**
- âœ… Easiest to amend
- âœ… Like ordinary legislation
- âœ… No special majority needed
- âŒ Not considered constitutional amendment under Art 368

**Example:** 100th Amendment Act, 2015 - Land Boundary Agreement with Bangladesh

---

## TYPE 2: Special Majority of Parliament

**Process:**
- Bill passed in each House by **special majority**
- Special majority means:
  - Majority of **total membership** of House, AND
  - 2/3rd majority of members **present and voting**
- President's assent required
- Most common type

**Calculation Example:**
- Total Lok Sabha strength: 545
- Members present: 400
- For special majority need:
  - At least 273 votes (majority of total 545), AND
  - At least 267 votes (2/3 of 400 present)
- So minimum required: **273 votes**

**Matters Amended (Examples):**
- Fundamental Rights (Part III)
- Directive Principles (Part IV)
- All matters not covered in Type 1 and Type 3
- Supreme Court & High Courts powers
- Union-State relations
- Creation of new states (Art 3)
- Ninth Schedule
- Election Commission
- CAG provisions
- Emergency provisions

**Key Characteristics:**
- âœ… Most provisions amended this way
- âœ… Considerable flexibility
- âŒ No state ratification needed
- âœ… Both Houses must pass separately

**Example:** 42nd Amendment (1976) - Mini Constitution

---

## TYPE 3: Special Majority + State Ratification

**Process:**
- Bill passed in each House by **special majority** (same as Type 2)
- **THEN** ratified by **at least half of state legislatures**
- State legislatures pass by **simple majority**
- No time limit for state ratification
- President's assent after ratification
- **Most rigid procedure**

**Matters Requiring State Ratification:**

Remember acronym: **"FUSED HAM"**

**F** - **Federal structure** (Art 368 itself)
**U** - **Union's executive power** (Art 73)
**S** - **State's executive power** (Art 162)
**E** - **Election of President** (Art 54, 55)
**D** - **Distribution of legislative powers** (7th Schedule)

**H** - **High Courts** - except provisions in Union List (Art 241, Ch V Part VI)
**A** - **Amendment procedure** (Art 368 itself)
**M** - **Matters in Union & State Lists** (Lists I & II)

**Additional provisions:**
- Representation of states in Parliament (Art 4, First Schedule)
- Supreme Court powers & constitution
- Extension of executive power of Union to states (Art 258)

**Key Characteristics:**
- âœ… Most difficult to amend
- âœ… Protects federal character
- âœ… States have veto power
- âœ… No time limit for ratification
- âŒ States cannot initiate amendment

## 10. Governor's Mandatory Reservation for Presidential Consideration

### Constitutional Provision: Article 200, Article 201

#### ðŸŽ¯ Key Points for Prelims

**What is Reservation of Bills?**
- Governor can **reserve** certain bills for President's consideration
- Bill does NOT become law immediately
- Requires **President's assent**
- Additional check on state legislation

---

### ðŸ“‹ Types of Reservation

## A. MANDATORY RESERVATION

**Bills that MUST be reserved:**

**1. Bills affecting High Court Powers (Art 200, Proviso):**
- Any bill that:
  - Diminishes powers of High Court
  - Affects HC's position/authority
  - **Must be reserved** for President

**2. Bills in Violation of Central Laws:**
- If bill passed by state legislature
- Contradicts existing Central law
- In Concurrent List subject

**3. Bills related to Taxation of Water/Electricity:**
- If law for taxation of:
  - Water supply
  - Electricity consumption/sale
- Reserved for President

**4. Bills affecting National Interest:**
- As directed by President
- Under Presidential directions

---

## B. DISCRETIONARY RESERVATION

**Governor MAY reserve (Art 200):**
- Any other bill if he thinks fit
- Uses discretionary power
- No binding rules
- Governor's judgment

**Common situations for discretionary reservation:**
- Bills against Directive Principles
- Bills with doubtful constitutional validity
- Bills affecting all-India services
- Bills causing inter-state complications
- Bills on sensitive subjects

---

### ðŸŽ¯ Presidential Action on Reserved Bills (Art 201)

**President has THREE options:**

**1. Give Assent:**
- Bill becomes law
- Governor promulgates

**2. Withhold Assent:**
- Bill does NOT become law
- Ends there
- State Legislature cannot override

**3. Return Bill for Reconsideration:**
- With or without message
- State Legislature reconsiders
- If passed again (with/without amendments)
- **Must be reserved again**
- President decides again
- NO TIME LIMIT for President

---

### ðŸ“Š Comparison: Governor's Four Options vs Reserved Bill

**When bill first comes to Governor (Art 200):**

| Option | Action | Effect |
|--------|--------|--------|
| 1. Give Assent | Signs bill | Becomes law |
| 2. Withhold Assent | Refuses | Bill dies |
| 3. Return Bill | Sends back | Legislature reconsiders |
| 4. Reserve Bill | To President | President decides |

**When bill reserved to President (Art 201):**

| Option | Action | Effect |
|--------|--------|--------|
| 1. Give Assent | Approves | Becomes law |
| 2. Withhold Assent | Refuses | Bill dies (final) |
| 3. Return for Reconsider | Sends back | Must be reserved again if passed |

---

### ðŸ’¡ Key Differences

**Withholding Assent:**

**By Governor:**
- If returned & re-passed, Governor **must give assent**
- Cannot withhold again (Art 200)
- Legislature has last word

**By President:**
- If withholds assent, bill **dies permanently**
- No override possible
- President has last word
- Pocket veto possible

---

### ðŸŽ¯ Pocket Veto

**By Governor:**
- Cannot exercise pocket veto
- Must act on bill
- Constitutional obligation

**By President:**
- **CAN exercise pocket veto**
- No time limit specified
- Can keep bill pending indefinitely
- Effectively kills bill without rejection

---

### ðŸ’¡ Brainstorming Points

**Why Mandatory Reservation?**
- Protect **High Court's authority**
- Ensure uniformity in taxation (water/electricity)
- Prevent violation of Central laws
- Safeguard national interests

**Federal Balance:**
- President's power checks state overreach
- Prevents unconstitutional state laws
- Maintains supremacy of Constitution
- Protects all-India interests

**Controversial Aspects:**
- Delays state legislation
- Reduces state autonomy
- President may sit on bills indefinitely
- Political misuse possible

**Important Cases:**

**1. Hoechst Pharmaceuticals vs State of Bihar (1983):**
- Governor must apply mind independently
- Cannot act on executive advice alone
- For reservation decision

**2. Shamsher Singh vs State of Punjab (1974):**
- Governor acts on aid & advice
- For assent/withholding
- Not for reservation

**3. Nabam Rebia Case (2016):**
- Governor cannot reserve money bills
- Special procedure for money bills exists

---

### ðŸ“‹ Special Categories

**1. Money Bills:**
- Special procedure (Art 199)
- Governor's recommendation required (Art 207)
- Different from ordinary bills
- Cannot be reserved to President

**2. Bills affecting Tribal Areas:**
- Governor must consult Tribes Advisory Council
- Additional safeguard
- Then normal procedure

**3. Bills by Legislative Council:**
- If rejected by Assembly twice
- Or delayed beyond specific period
- Joint sitting not allowed (unlike Parliament)
